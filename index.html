01 About Me

Introduction &

Values

Growing upI was always interested in how things worked. My grandfather used to take us to the dump in his small town, find “broken” bikes, lawnmowers, and furniture, and bring them back to his shop to fix. Sometimes he would take parts from multiple bikes just to build one that we could ride all summer. He believed most things weren’t truly broken, but people just didn’t know how to fix them. My dad also has a similar mindset. He has a full workshop where he has built furniture, worked on trucks, and repaired old appliances. In that environment, I naturally started taking things apart too. Fixing toys, woodworking, and eventually repairing electronics. In school, my choice classes always focused on engineering and computing.

Across my careerI have used this mindset repeatedly. From working as a technician to teaching, and now building projects in analytics and development. I’ve always approached problems by trying to understand how things work at a deeper level, breaking them down, and rebuilding them into something more functional and easier to use. Teaching especially changed how I think. It forced me to consider how different people approach problems and how to present complex ideas in a way that actually makes sense to them. I learned to break concepts down to manageable sizes, and that solving a problem isn’t just about getting the right answer, it’s about making the solution usable and understandable for others.

Outside of workmost of my time is spent with my wife, Jenny, and our son Sully. We like to take things easy by getting outside for sports, playing video games, and relaxing to end the day. I still spend time working on projects like remodeling our basement and fixing vintage electronics. Recently, I restored a 70s pachinko machine by re-designing and 3D-printing a replacement part that couldn’t be found anywhere. Another time, while playing Mario Kart together, Sully asked me why he couldn’t drive in deep mud. I explained how, in the real world, mud would slow you down, but I also explained how the game code has conditions and interactions to handle how we play. I admit it was probably over my 5-year-old's head, but he’s a curious learner.

At this pointI’m focused on continuing to grow and spend more time where I can design, create, and improve projects over time.
